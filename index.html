903 Strangers' Livers

What can you learn from a pile of unlabeled genomic data? Someone handed us a 79-megabyte file and a one-sentence description. Here's what the data said when we asked it to speak for itself.

Act I

A blind exploration

First we pretend we know nothing. The only fact we allow ourselves is the filename: human_liver.tsv. Everything else we'll make the data prove.

Someone handed you a file called human_liver.tsv. That's all you know about it. It's 79 megabytes. When you unzip it and open it, you find this:

No names. No diagnoses. No timepoints. No tissue sub-region. No demographics. No treatment. Just 31.8 million numbers and a promise that a liver was involved.

This is more normal than it sounds. Most public RNA-seq data on Earth looks exactly like this: a giant rectangular matrix with opaque sample IDs and whatever story the original scientists remembered to upload alongside it. Sometimes that story is rich. Often it's missing.

We're going to work through the data the way a careful bioinformatician would on a first date with a new dataset — not to prove a hypothesis, but to find out what questions are even askable. Think of it as an exploratory diagnostic.

A very large pile of numbers

The numbers are not gentle. They range from zero to 9.4 million. Most of them are zero — 61.7% of the matrix is empty. The ones that aren't zero span seven orders of magnitude. And the column totals vary wildly: the quietest sample has 107,000 total reads; the loudest has 132 million. That's a 1,236× difference in raw signal.

Before we can compare samples to each other, we have to do something about this. That something is called normalization and it's the most important pre-flight check in the whole workflow. But first, a quick sanity question.

What genes are loudest?

Let's start with the dumbest possible question: when we sort every gene in the file by median expression across all 903 samples, what floats to the top?

The very top of the list is dominated by mitochondrial genes (MT-CO1, MT-ND4, MT-CYB) and ribosomal housekeepers. That's expected — they're always loud in bulk RNA-seq because every cell makes tons of them. It's what comes next that's interesting.

Eleven of the top 50 most-expressed genes are textbook hepatocyte-secretome markers: ALB (albumin, the most abundant protein in your blood), SERPINA1 (alpha-1 antitrypsin), TF (transferrin), HP (haptoglobin), APOB (apolipoprotein B, the backbone of LDL), the fibrinogens (FGA, FGB, FGG), APOA1, APOA2, and TTR. These are the proteins hepatocytes are famous for manufacturing and shipping into the bloodstream.

We haven't used the filename yet. We've only asked the data which genes it's loudest about. And the data just confirmed it's liver.

But hidden inside that reassuring answer is a loose thread: ALB's minimum value across these 903 samples is zero. At least one of these "liver" samples has no detectable albumin mRNA at all. That's not a liver cell. Hold onto that thread; we'll pull it in Act II.

Why raw counts lie

Here are two of those 903 samples — picked because one has a tiny library (339,099 total reads) and the other has a huge one (52,709,047 total reads). That's a 155× difference in sequencing depth. If you compared their albumin counts directly…

That transformation — divide by the library size, multiply by a million, add one, take the log — is three lines of code. It's also the single most consequential decision in RNA-seq preprocessing. Every comparison you make after this point lives or dies on whether you normalized honestly.

With the samples now comparable, we can ask bigger questions.

Let the data arrange itself

We have 903 samples and a normalized expression value for every gene. Each sample is therefore a single point in a 35,238-dimensional space — a geometry no brain has ever intuited. But most of those dimensions are junk: genes that don't vary between samples carry no information. So we keep only the 2,000 most variable genes and ask a linear-algebra question:

Of all possible flat 2D projections of this 2,000-dimensional cloud, which one preserves the most separation between points?

The answer is called the first two principal components. On this dataset: PC1 captures 36% of the total variance in the data, and PC2 captures another 14%. Together, a single flat picture captures half the story.

What's actually in the axes

PCA is a machine for making low-dimensional pictures, but each axis is also a recipe — a weighted sum of specific genes. The genes with the largest absolute weight on PC1 are the genes that define what it means to move from the left side of the picture to the right side. Look at which ones show up for PC1 and PC2 below, then click a gene name to recolor the scatter by that gene's expression and see which direction it pulls.

Act II

The answer key

We've been pretending we don't know anything about these samples. That's a useful pedagogical lie — it makes you ask better questions — but it's still a lie. Let's break it.

Every one of these 903 samples has a real name in a public database: the GEO sample accession (GSM…). NCBI publishes a rich JSON record for each accession — the original study it came from, the experimenter's notes, often a tissue source and a disease state. We wrote a Python script, fetched metadata for all 903 accessions with polite rate limits, and cached the result.

Then came the part that turned out to matter most. The first pass at tagging each sample used regex pattern-matching on the free-text titles and summaries — the kind of "if title contains 'HCC' then primary_tag = HCC" rules that look reasonable until you actually audit them. The audit found errors in 53 of the 60 source studies, so we replaced the regex with three LLM sub-agents that read every study's full text and applied a controlled vocabulary by hand. That still wasn't good enough — many studies contain multiple sample groups with different biology (the iPSC liver-bud paper also contributes its primary fetal and adult hepatocyte references), and ARCHS4 keyword-searches at the sample level, not the study level. So we did the audit a second time at the per-sample-group granularity. The categories below are the final LLM-curated calls, with one tag per unique sample description within each study.

Were the clusters right?

Now for the moment of truth. We're going to take the PCA scatter from earlier and recolor it — first by the study each sample came from, then by the disease tag we extracted from the metadata. If our blind clustering matched real biology, we should see structure when we recolor.

Welcome to RNA-seq's oldest problem. A lot of the structure we saw in the PCA is batch effect — samples cluster by who ran the experiment as much as by what the experiment was studying. A study that used poly-A selection will look systematically different from one that used ribosomal depletion, even if the underlying biology is identical. A study run in 2014 will differ from one run in 2020 because the sequencing chemistry changed.

Real biological signal is still in there — fetal-liver samples (remember the albumin-zero thread from earlier? It turns out 217 of the 903 samples are fetal liver tissue, not adult) form a massive cluster far from the adult tissue samples; HCC drifts in one direction; normal adult livers in another. But it's tangled with the batch structure, and untangling the two is where the actual analytical work lives.

The honest lesson of this dataset is that a PCA reveals structure, not meaning. The meaning comes from knowing what you were measuring.

A different lens: UMAP

PCA is a linear projection. It asks: of all possible flat rotations of this 2,000-dimensional cloud, which one preserves the most variance? That makes the resulting axes globally interpretable — PC1 is a real direction in gene-expression space — but it also means PCA can only capture relationships that can be summarised by straight lines.

A non-linear projection like UMAP asks a different question: which samples are nearest each other in the original 2,000-dimensional space, and how can we lay them out on a plane so that nearest-neighbour relationships are preserved? UMAP often makes clusters look crisper than PCA does — but it pays for that with a brutal distortion of distance between clusters. Two UMAP blobs that look far apart might be neighbours; two that look adjacent might not be.

Here's the same 903 samples, the same 2,000 most-variable genes, the same z-scoring — but projected with UMAP instead. Try the same recolouring buttons. The blobs are tighter, the boundaries between cell-line / organoid / tissue / tumour are sharper, and the same batch-effect-vs-biology tension you saw in the PCA shows up here in a slightly different shape.

Neither projection is "the" answer. PCA tells you the dominant axes of variation; UMAP tells you which samples are one another's neighbours. Both are diagnostic, neither is truth, and the discipline is in knowing what each one is for.

How this was made

This entire analysis — the Python pipeline, the normalization and PCA, the scrollytelling front-end, the colour palette, the prose you just read — was built in a single conversation with Claude Code, a terminal-native coding agent.

What the agent did on its own

  • Explored the demo folder, read dataset_info.md, extracted the zip and profiled the 79 MB TSV.
  • Wrote a Python pipeline using NumPy, pandas and scikit-learn for log-CPM normalization, top-variance gene selection, and PCA on 903 samples.
  • Wrote a second script to fetch GEO metadata for all 903 samples via NCBI E-utilities, with batching, rate limiting, a disk cache, and resumable failure handling.
  • Computed a UMAP projection of the same matrix using umap-learn and rendered it side-by-side with PCA so the contrast in geometry is visible.
  • Tagged the 903 samples by biological category in three escalating passes: first a regex heuristic (wrong on 53 of 60 studies), then LLM sub-agents reading study-level text (wrong on the multi-condition studies because ARCHS4 keyword-searches at the sample level), then LLM sub-agents reading per-sample-group text (86 unique groups, hand-classified by reading the title, summary and experimental design of each).
  • Wrote this HTML, CSS and JavaScript — layout, interactions, scroll-triggered animations, the brush-to-select behaviour, all of it.

Where the human steered

  • Chose the dataset (RNA-seq, not the plate reader).
  • Named the audience (PhD researchers who are not necessarily biologists).
  • Pointed at the ASAP brand reference URL.
  • Approved the two-act narrative structure before any code was written.
  • Picked the shorter, punchier title.

The punchline is not that an AI can replace the scientist. It's that the scientist can now ask "what would happen if I looked at this a different way?" — and have the answer in minutes instead of weeks. Exploratory analysis used to be expensive. It isn't anymore. The question is what you do with all that newly cheap curiosity.
